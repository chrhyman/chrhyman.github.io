What is a wug?

Just a cute cartoon, or a test of psycholinguistic ability?

Why do linguists care about this bird?

One of the questions that deeply interests linguists is that of how children acquire Language. The idea of an infant getting such a small amount of input but developing a system of communication that is robust and adaptable is nothing short of astounding. As linguistics is a science like any other, one must attempt to follow the experimental method to provide empirical proof of theories about language development.

The “Wug Test” was developed by Dr. Jean Berko Gleason in 19581 as a psycholinguistic test of a child’s learning of English morphemes. The idea, in a nutshell, is to present a child with a brand new word and see if they can successfully modify it to follow English’s rules of allomorphy and pronunciation.

1 Berko, J. (1958). The child’s learning of English morphology. WORD, 14(2-3), 150-177. DOI.
